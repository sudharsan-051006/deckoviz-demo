# A List Of The 100 Most Iconic Fiction Books

Suggestion: Visualize your favourite fiction.

Fiction has always been more than storytelling. These novels endure because they reveal something essential about human nature — our longing, cruelty, hope, fear, love, and imagination. Across centuries and cultures, these works continue to shape how we understand the world and ourselves.

<br>

1. Pride and Prejudice by Jane Austen<br>
A witty and incisive exploration of class, marriage, and moral growth in 19th-century England, blending romance with social critique.

<br>

2. To Kill a Mockingbird by Harper Lee<br>
A deeply humane novel confronting racial injustice, empathy, and moral courage through a child’s perspective.

<br>

3. 1984 by George Orwell<br>
A chilling dystopia depicting surveillance, propaganda, and the erosion of truth under totalitarian power.

<br>

4. The Great Gatsby by F. Scott Fitzgerald<br>
A tragic meditation on illusion, desire, and the emptiness beneath the American Dream.

<br>

5. Moby-Dick by Herman Melville<br>
An epic struggle between man and nature that explores obsession, fate, and the unknowable.

<br>

6. One Hundred Years of Solitude by Gabriel García Márquez<br>
A landmark of magical realism tracing generations of a family where myth and history merge.

<br>

7. Ulysses by James Joyce<br>
A revolutionary modernist novel transforming a single day into an epic of consciousness and language.

<br>

8. The Lord of the Rings by J.R.R. Tolkien<br>
A foundational fantasy epic exploring friendship, sacrifice, power, and the nature of evil.

<br>

9. Jane Eyre by Charlotte Brontë<br>
A gothic coming-of-age novel about independence, morality, and emotional integrity.

<br>

10. Wuthering Heights by Emily Brontë<br>
A dark and elemental tale of passion, obsession, and revenge shaped by landscape and emotion.

<br>

11. Little Women by Louisa May Alcott<br>
A tender portrayal of family, ambition, and womanhood during the American Civil War.

<br>

12. Crime and Punishment by Fyodor Dostoevsky<br>
A profound psychological study of guilt, conscience, and redemption.

<br>

13. Catch-22 by Joseph Heller<br>
A satirical dismantling of war, bureaucracy, and institutional absurdity.

<br>

14. Beloved by Toni Morrison<br>
A haunting exploration of memory, trauma, and the enduring scars of slavery.

<br>

15. The Catcher in the Rye by J.D. Salinger<br>
A defining portrait of teenage alienation and emotional vulnerability.

<br>

16. Slaughterhouse-Five by Kurt Vonnegut<br>
An anti-war novel blending science fiction, fatalism, and dark humor.

<br>

17. Brave New World by Aldous Huxley<br>
A dystopian vision of a society controlled through pleasure and conformity.

<br>

18. The Handmaid’s Tale by Margaret Atwood<br>
A stark warning about authoritarianism, gender oppression, and loss of autonomy.

<br>

19. Frankenstein by Mary Shelley<br>
A foundational work questioning creation, responsibility, and what it means to be human.

<br>

20. The Picture of Dorian Gray by Oscar Wilde<br>
A decadent tale of vanity, corruption, and moral decay beneath beauty.

<br>

21. The Adventures of Huckleberry Finn by Mark Twain<br>
A powerful critique of racism and hypocrisy through a river journey.

<br>

22. Anna Karenina by Leo Tolstoy<br>
A sweeping examination of love, betrayal, and societal constraint.

<br>

23. Lolita by Vladimir Nabokov<br>
A controversial yet stylistically brilliant exploration of obsession and manipulation.

<br>

24. The Color Purple by Alice Walker<br>
A story of survival, voice, and resilience against systemic oppression.

<br>

25. Mrs. Dalloway by Virginia Woolf<br>
A modernist exploration of memory, trauma, and inner life.

<br>

26. The Sound and the Fury by William Faulkner<br>
An experimental narrative shaped by time, memory, and decay.

<br>

27. Invisible Man by Ralph Ellison<br>
A groundbreaking exploration of identity and invisibility in America.

<br>

28. Middlemarch by George Eliot<br>
A rich portrait of provincial life and moral complexity.

<br>

29. The Brothers Karamazov by Fyodor Dostoevsky<br>
A philosophical exploration of faith, doubt, free will, and morality.

<br>

30. The Things They Carried by Tim O’Brien<br>
Interlinked stories examining truth, memory, and the emotional weight of war.

<br>

31. The Hitchhiker’s Guide to the Galaxy by Douglas Adams<br>
A cosmic satire blending science fiction with existential humor.

<br>

32. Dune by Frank Herbert<br>
A monumental science fiction epic about power, ecology, and destiny.

<br>

33. Harry Potter series by J.K. Rowling<br>
A cultural phenomenon exploring friendship, loss, and moral choice.

<br>

34. The Book Thief by Markus Zusak<br>

<br>

<br>

<br>

<br>

53. Life of Pi by Yann Martel<br>
A philosophical survival tale blending faith and imagination.

<br>

54. The Brief Wondrous Life of Oscar Wao by Junot Díaz<br>
A vibrant exploration of identity, history, and inheritance.

<br>

55. Midnight’s Children by Salman Rushdie<br>
A magical realist chronicle of post-colonial India.

<br>

56. Never Let Me Go by Kazuo Ishiguro<br>
A haunting meditation on memory, mortality, and humanity.

<br>

57. The Poisonwood Bible by Barbara Kingsolver<br>
A powerful critique of colonialism and faith.

<br>

58. Cloud Atlas by David Mitchell<br>
Interconnected narratives spanning centuries and genres.

<br>

60. The Remains of the Day by Kazuo Ishiguro<br>
A quiet tragedy of repression and regret.

<br>

61. Possession by A.S. Byatt<br>

A story of healing through place and community.

<br>

64. The Curious Incident of the Dog in the Night-Time by Mark Haddon<br>
A unique narrative exploring logic, perception, and empathy.

<br>

65. The Corrections by Jonathan Franzen<br>
A sharp portrait of family and modern anxiety.

<br>

66. Gilead by Marilynne Robinson<br>
A reflective meditation on faith, memory, and legacy.

76. The Once and Future King by T.H. White<br>
A reflective retelling of Arthurian legend.

An epic adventure told through animal mythology.

78. I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings by Maya Angelou<br>
An autobiographical narrative of voice and survival.

79. The Mists of Avalon by Marion Zimmer Bradley<br>

81. Jonathan Strange & Mr Norrell by Susanna Clarke<br>

83. American Gods by Neil Gaiman<br>
A modern myth blending belief, identity, and culture.

84. Good Omens by Terry Pratchett and Neil Gaiman<br>
A humorous take on apocalypse and humanity.

<br>

85. The Night Watch by Sarah Waters<br>
A wartime exploration of love, secrecy, and identity.

<br>

86. Fingersmith by Sarah Waters<br>
A Victorian crime novel full of deception and reversal.

<br>

87. The Secret History by Donna Tartt<br>
A dark academic novel about obsession and guilt.

<br>

88. Middlesex by Jeffrey Eugenides<br>
A multi-generational story of identity and transformation.

<br>

89. Atonement by Ian McEwan<br>
A meditation on memory, guilt, and storytelling.

<br>

90. The Sympathizer by Viet Thanh Nguyen<br>
A sharp and unsettling novel exploring identity, loyalty, and the contradictions of war through the eyes of a divided narrator.

<br>

91. The Stranger by Albert Camus<br>
A stark existential novel exploring absurdity, detachment, and the tension between individual truth and social expectation. Through its emotionally distant protagonist, the novel questions morality, meaning, and the human response to an indifferent universe.

<br>

92. Don Quixote by Miguel de Cervantes<br>
Often considered the first modern novel, this satirical yet deeply human story explores idealism, madness, and the clash between imagination and reality. Don Quixote’s quest exposes both the beauty and danger of believing too deeply in illusions.

<br>

93. The Trial by Franz Kafka<br>
A nightmarish vision of bureaucracy and power, this novel depicts an individual trapped in a system without logic or mercy. It captures modern anxieties about authority, guilt, and the impossibility of justice.

<br>

94. The Sun Also Rises by Ernest Hemingway<br>
A defining novel of the Lost Generation, portraying emotional disillusionment and quiet longing after World War I. Beneath its restrained prose lies a profound meditation on love, masculinity, and meaning.

<br>

95. Love in the Time of Cholera by Gabriel García Márquez<br>
A sweeping exploration of love across decades, examining patience, obsession, and devotion. The novel challenges conventional ideas of romance and celebrates love’s persistence beyond youth and reason.

<br>

96. The Metamorphosis by Franz Kafka<br>
A surreal and unsettling transformation that becomes a metaphor for alienation, family obligation, and identity. Kafka exposes how society treats those who no longer fit expected roles.

<br>

97. The Alchemist by Paulo Coelho<br>
A symbolic and philosophical journey centered on destiny, self-discovery, and listening to one’s inner calling. Its simplicity conceals a universal message about purpose and faith in life’s path.

<br>

98. The Little Prince by Antoine de Saint-Exupéry<br>
A deceptively simple tale blending childhood wonder with profound philosophical insight. Through allegory, it reflects on love, loss, responsibility, and what truly matters.

<br>

99. The Handmaid’s Tale by Margaret Atwood<br>
A dystopian exploration of power, control, and resistance, examining how language, memory, and storytelling become tools of survival in oppressive systems.

<br>

100. War and Peace by Leo Tolstoy<br>
A monumental literary achievement that blends personal lives with sweeping historical forces. Tolstoy explores love, war, faith, free will, and the illusion of control, offering a deeply human vision of history and existence.
